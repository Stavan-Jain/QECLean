### 1c. Why these are not coincidental restrictions

The BBCS algorithm uses three semisimplicity-dependent facts:

1. **Generating idempotent**: in Section II ("R is semisimple"
   precondition for *every* ideal to be generated by an
   idempotent), the framework treats any abelian code as
   `C = eA_q(r_1,…,r_s)` for some `e = e^2 ∈ A_q(r_1,…,r_s)`.
   In a non-semisimple ring, ideals are generated by elements but
   not in general by idempotents (e.g. `F_2[Z_2] = F_2[x]/(x^2-1)
   = F_2[x]/((x+1)^2)`; the ideal generated by `(x+1)` is not
   generated by any idempotent because it is contained in the
   Jacobson radical and is itself nilpotent).

2. **Discrete Fourier transform** (Page 2, last paragraph):
   "*the function Fourier transform may be viewed as an isomorphism
   of algebras φ_α: A_q(r_1,…,r_s) → (F_{q^v}^{|I|}, ⋆), where the
   multiplication ⋆ in F_{q^v}^{|I|} is defined coordinatewise.*"
   This is exactly Wedderburn-Artin for the semisimple case. For
   `gcd(|G|, q) > 1` the right-hand side has more than direct-product
   structure — there are non-trivial local-ring factors with
   Jacobson radical — and there is no isomorphism of this form.

3. **Theorem 12 / Definition 9** (Page 5, "Apparent distance of an
   abelian code"): "We recall that A_q(r_1,…,r_s) is a semisimple
   ring; **every ideal is generated by an idempotent which decomposes
   as sum of primitive central idempotents.**" This is the structural
   foundation of `d^*(C) = max_α { mad(M(φ_{α,e})) : α ∈ U }`. In a
   non-semisimple ring, the analogous decomposition `1 = e_1 + ... +
   e_k` of the unit into primitive central idempotents does not exist
   (it would imply semisimplicity by definition).

So the assumption is not a notational convenience that could be
relaxed by a generalization; it is woven into Theorems 12, 22, 25
of the paper.

## 3. The single instance in scope (bb_90_8_10)

bb_90 is the only Bravyi code with F_2[G] semisimple (|G|=45=3²·5,
gcd(45, 2) = 1). For bb_90:

- G = Z_15 × Z_3 (decomposition `Z_45 ↔ Z_15 × Z_3` since
  gcd(15, 3) = 3, so this is the canonical factorization; not Z_45
  even though |G| = 45).
- G is non-cyclic (lcm(15, 3) = 15 < 45), so classical univariate
  HT does not apply.
- BBCS *would* apply here in principle.

Round 3's HT bound returned 1 for bb_90 (per T2R3.4): mv_ht=1
because the non-cyclic-G_odd obstruction also applies (no full-G
generator step). BBCS, with its hypermatrix machinery, could
plausibly improve on this. **But:**

- bb_90's actual `d = 10`. To beat Round 3 the BBCS bound on bb_90
  alone would need to be ≥ 8 (per the T2R4 spec's Round 3 threshold)
  — and bb_90 alone is not the engineering target.
- Even if BBCS gave a tight bound of 10 on bb_90, this would not
  lift any obstruction for the four non-semisimple Bravyi instances
  (gross, bb_72, bb_108, bb_288).
- The program's ultimate goal is gross (and beyond, to the
  asymptotic `d ≈ √n` scaling family). A bound that fires on bb_90
  but not gross is a small-step result, not a Tier-2 advance.

This is the analogous-to-Round-3 outcome from one perspective: even
if implemented, BBCS would produce a "survives-but-trivial-on-gross"
verdict (per the T2R4 verdict tree), and the spec instructs
"shelve" in that branch too.

## 4. Companion literature confirming the obstruction

### 4a. Jitman, Ling 2013 — explicit non-semisimple case

**Source**: "Abelian Codes in Principal Ideal Group Algebras,"
IEEE TIT 59(1), 211-222 (2013). doi:10.1109/TIT.2012.2215576.

This paper *explicitly* addresses the non-semisimple case: a PIGA
(principal ideal group algebra) is a group algebra F[G] where every
ideal is principal, which in the abelian case characterizes
algebras of the form `F[H] × F[G/Sylow_p(G)]` where H is the Sylow
p-subgroup.

Two findings relevant to our program:

1. **Upper bound from semisimple quotient (not improvement):** The
   paper's Proposition 4.4 gives an upper bound on `d` for abelian
   codes in non-semisimple PIGAs in terms of the minimum distance
   of abelian codes in the *semisimple quotient* `F[G/Sylow_p(G)]`.
   Specifically `d_non-ss(C) ≤ d_ss(π(C))` where π is the projection
   to the semisimple quotient. So non-semisimple bounds are derived
   from, and bounded above by, semisimple bounds — never an
   improvement.

2. **Asymptotic badness:** Theorem 5.1 shows abelian codes in
   non-semisimple PIGAs are asymptotically bad, in the sense that
   `d/n → 0` as `n → ∞` for any sequence of such codes (analogous
   to the classical repeated-root cyclic code result).

This is not a "missing paper" — it is the explicit literature
statement that non-semisimple-abelian-code distance is **not**
where one looks for tight bounds in the asymptotic regime. The BB
program wants tight distance on `n = 144` (gross) and asymptotically
`d ≈ √n`, but non-semisimple-abelian-code distance asymptotics are
provably worse.

### 4b. Camion 1971 — original framework, semisimple-only

**Source**: P. Camion, "Abelian Codes," MRC Tech. Sum. Rep. #1059,
U. Wisconsin (1971). Not online (pre-arXiv); cited via BBCS 2016
and Sabin-Lomonaco 1992.

The original definition of "apparent distance" for abelian codes
operates over the character group of G, which is well-defined iff
F_q contains a primitive `lcm(r_i)`-th root of unity. This requires
`gcd(|G|, q) = 1` (otherwise the relevant characters don't exist over
any extension of F_q in a usable way for the DFT construction).

### 4c. Roth-Seroussi, Castagnoli et al. — repeated-root cyclic codes

Cited as background. The univariate (s=1) analogue of the
non-semisimple abelian case is the *repeated-root* cyclic code:
`F_q[x]/(x^n - 1)` with `gcd(n, q) > 1`. Roth-Seroussi (1986) and
Castagnoli-Massey-Schoeller-von Seemann (1991) show such codes
have `d = d_simple-root-reduction × repetition-count`, again
revealing that non-semisimple distance is structurally derivative,
not novel.

## 5. The compounding-obstruction observation, strengthened

HANDOFF §6i identifies three obstructions for the engineering
target:

1. F_2[G] non-semisimple.
2. G_odd non-cyclic.
3. Degenerate supports (`[G:⟨supp(A)⟩] > 1`).

Round 3 attempted to address (2) by implementing multivariate-HT.
The implementation gave trivial bounds because **(1) and (2)
compound**: classical-cyclic-code algebra requires (1) (semisimple)
AND (in the cyclic G case) ¬(2). When both fail, no classical bound
fires nontrivially.

Round 4 was supposed to address (2) by escalating to BBCS, which
handles non-cyclic G via hypermatrices. The literature check
reveals that **BBCS still requires (1)**: the framework operates
over the semisimple group algebra `A_q(r_1,…,r_s)` and inherits the
Camion 1971 / Wedderburn-Artin assumption.

So **the entire BBCS-line (Camion 1971 → Sabin-Lomonaco 1992 →
Saints-Heegard 1995 → BBCS 2016 → BGS 2017) addresses obstruction
(2) but not (1).** For BB codes' engineering target, where both
obstructions are active, the entire line is silent.

This is a stronger version of Round 3's lesson: the literature
gap is not "BBCS is missing" or "we haven't found the right paper
yet." The literature gap is "**the character-theoretic /
Fourier-transform / apparent-distance line of multivariate
abelian-code distance bounds is structurally incompatible with
non-semisimple F_q[G]**." Any future Tier-2 candidate that wants
to bound gross has to step outside this entire research line.
