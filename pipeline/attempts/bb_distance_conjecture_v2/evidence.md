## Falsified candidates: violation analysis

### `r2_tz_joint_per_orbit_lower` (115 violations)

Mechanism of failure: the joint per-orbit kernel min weight is
**strictly larger than `min_wt_ker_A` in general** because
cross-orbit cancellation is forbidden by the per-orbit constraint.
The bound's numerator is thus larger than the textbook upper bound
`min(d_A^⊥, d_B^⊥)`, allowing the divided-by-c bound to exceed
`d_X`.

Worst violation:

```
Row 5632c6ff: G = Z_3 × Z_6, A = 1 + y + y^2, B = 1 + x + x^2
d_exact = 2, bound = 8 (violates by +6)
```

Mechanism: `min_O d_O^joint(A, B) = 16` (approximately, when both
A and B vanish on multiple orbits and the joint kernel restricted
per-orbit has weight 16), `c = 2`, so bound = 8. But `d_X = 2`
because A and B share x-axis structure that admits a weight-2
cross-orbit logical.

Per-group violations:
- Z3xZ3: 1
- Z3xZ4: 7
- Z3xZ5: 11
- Z3xZ6: 15
- Z4xZ6: 15
- Z5xZ6: 27
- Z6xZ6: 39

The violation rate increases with `|G|` because larger groups have
more orbits, more chances for cross-orbit cancellation, and thus
more inflation of the per-orbit numerator.

### `r2_support_indicator_lower` (3068 violations, 79.7%)

Mechanism of failure: replacing `c = |G_a ∩ G_b|` with the number
of joint vanishing orbits `|V_A ∩ V_B|` is heuristic and has no
proof. For most corpus codes, `|V_A ∩ V_B|` is much smaller than
`c`, so the bound is much larger than TZ, and is essentially never
correct as a lower bound.

This candidate is included as a cautionary example of "what NOT to
do" — it directly violates the rule that the denominator must have
a defensible derivation, not just be a structural count picked at
random.

## Recommended candidate: `r2_tz_or_girth_composite`

Of the 5 survivors:
- `r2_TZ_lower_recomputed` and `r2_tz_safe_lower` are essentially
  identical (4 tight on corpus). Pure TZ.
- `r2_tanner_girth_lower` gives 457 tight (11.7%).
- `r2_tz_or_girth_composite` gives 458 tight (11.9%) — the max of
  TZ and girth, so weakly more tight than either alone.
- `r2_bch_per_axis_lower` is essentially trivial.

The composite candidate is the strongest survivor. It is **not new
mathematics** — it is just the maximum of two published lower
bounds. But it is the best engineering choice for "what number to
report" when a single lower bound is wanted.

**None of the survivors is tight on gross.** All give bound=2 on
gross's d=12.

## Open question for Tier 3

The gross polynomial `A = x^3 + y + y^2 ∈ F_2[Z_12 × Z_6]` has
support `{(3, 0), (0, 1), (0, 2)}` — three monomials with NO
overlap between the x-axis and y-axis. This "axis-orthogonal"
structure is what makes the BCH-per-axis bound fail (axes are
considered independently, missing the cross-axis interaction).

Any successful lower bound that closes the gap on gross **must
exploit this cross-axis interaction structure**. None of the
surviving candidates do. The Hartmann-Tzeng bound or the Roos
bound are published refinements of BCH that might handle this;
investigating them is the recommended Tier-3 step.
