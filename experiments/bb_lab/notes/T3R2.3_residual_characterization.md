# T3R2.3 — Structural characterization of non-degenerate Jacobson-bound violators

After filtering to non-degenerate BB codes (`⟨supp(A)⟩ = G = ⟨supp(B)⟩`),
the real Jacobson-radical bound

    bound = Σ_{O ∈ V_A ∩ V_B} |O| · min(μ_O(A), μ_O(B))

has **43 violations on 1937 rows (2.2%)**. This note dissects them.

---

## 1. Numerical homogeneity

Every violator has identical `(k, d_exact, bound, slack)`:

| group | rows | k | d_exact | jacobson_bound | slack |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Z3xZ5 | 8 | 8 | 2 | 4 | -2 |
| Z5xZ6 | 28 | 8 | 2 | 4 | -2 |
| Z6xZ6 | 7 | 8 | 2 | 4 | -2 |

* No spread in `d_exact`: all have weight-2 logicals.
* No spread in `bound`: all give the same RHS of 4.
* Slack is uniformly -2.
* `A_weight = B_weight = 3` in **all** 43 cases.

This is not a "tail" of violations bleeding off into wider gaps — it's
a sharply-localized failure mode at exactly the weight-2 logical
boundary.

---

## 2. Polynomial-pair structure

Only **four distinct `A` polynomials** account for all 43 violators
(modulo the corpus's canonical form for `(A, B)` pairs):

| `A_poly` | group | count |
|---|---|---:|
| `1 + x*y + x^2` | Z5xZ6 | 12 |
| `y^3 + x*y + x^2` | Z5xZ6 | 16 |
| `y^2 + y^3 + x` | Z3xZ5 | 8 |
| `1 + y + x` | Z6xZ6 | 7 |

The `B` polynomial varies across each row's canonical neighborhood,
but all `B`s either equal `A` literally (4 cases) or are
`monomial · A` translates of it (37 cases) or are
"weight-2-divisible" siblings of it (2 cases). See §3.

---

## 3. The 41/43 case: `B = (monomial) · A`

For **41 of 43 violators**, there exists `h ∈ G` such that
`B = x^h · A` as polynomials in `F_2[G]` (where `x^h` is the
group-algebra monomial corresponding to `h`).

Why this matters: the X-stabilizer rows of `H_X = (B^T | A^T)` (or
equivalently the kernel of `H_Z = (A | B)`) contains the pair
`(α, β) := (1, x^{-h})` since

    A · 1 + B · x^{-h} = A + (x^h · A) · x^{-h} = A + A = 0   (in F_2[G]).

This `(1, x^{-h})` is a weight-2 vector in `ker(H_Z)`. Whether it's a
nontrivial logical depends on whether it lies in `rowspan(H_X)`. In
each of the 41 violator rows, SAT confirmed `d_exact = 2`, so the
weight-2 codeword is *not* in the rowspan — it's a real X-logical.

**Concrete first 5 violators with their polynomial pairs and the
shift `h` certifying the unit-translate:**

| code_id | group | A | B | n | k | d | bound | shift `h` |
|---|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| bb_enum_Z3xZ5_4e4eeaa5 | Z3xZ5 | `y^2 + y^3 + x` | `y^2 + y^3 + x` | 30 | 8 | 2 | 4 | (0, 0) — `A = B` exactly |
| bb_enum_Z3xZ5_e23f912c | Z3xZ5 | `y^2 + y^3 + x` | `y^3 + y^4 + x*y` | 30 | 8 | 2 | 4 | (0, 1) — `B = y · A` |
| bb_enum_Z3xZ5_4c4ad087 | Z3xZ5 | `y^2 + y^3 + x` | `y^3 + x^2 + x^2*y` | 30 | 8 | 2 | 4 | (2, 1) — `B = x²y · A` |
| bb_enum_Z5xZ6_4ae40e76 | Z5xZ6 | `1 + x*y + x^2` | `1 + x*y + x^2` | 60 | 8 | 2 | 4 | (0, 0) — `A = B` exactly |
| bb_enum_Z5xZ6_1b54a031 | Z5xZ6 | `1 + x*y + x^2` | `y + x*y^2 + x^2*y` | 60 | 8 | 2 | 4 | (0, 1) — `B = y · A` |

The shift `h` was certified by brute-force enumeration of all `|G|`
candidates (`scripts/tier3_round2_eval.py` does not record it; the
verification is in `notes/T3R2.3_residual_characterization.md`'s
code probe below).

## 4. The 2/43 case: `B = monomial · (1 + x + x²)` on Z3-axis

The remaining 2 violators on Z_3 × Z_5:

| code_id | A | B | min_wt_ker_A | min_wt_ker_B |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|
| bb_enum_Z3xZ5_e07a228f | `y^2 + y^3 + x` | `y^2 + x*y^2 + x^2*y^2` | 8 | 2 |
| bb_enum_Z3xZ5_e2ac9d88 | `y^2 + y^3 + x` | `y + x*y + x^2*y` | 8 | 2 |

Here `B = y^c · (1 + x + x²)` for `c ∈ {1, 2}`. The element
`(1 + x + x²) ∈ F_2[Z_3]` is a **zero-divisor**: in `F_2[t]/(t^3 - 1)`,

    (1 + t + t²) · (1 + t) = 1 + t + t² + t + t² + t³ = 1 + t³ = 0,

so `(1 + x + x²)` has a weight-2 dual codeword `(1 + x)`. Concretely,
`B · (1 + x) = 0` in F_2[G]. So `(0, 1 + x) ∈ ker(H_Z)` is a weight-2
X-vector; if it's not in `rowspan(H_X)`, it's a weight-2 X-logical.
SAT confirms `d = 2`, so it is.

This is the textbook CSS classical-dual upper bound saturating: any
BB code with `min_wt_ker_B = 2` has `d_X ≤ 2`. The 2 outliers fall
exactly here.

---

## 5. Unifying structural property: **weight-2 polynomial divisor**

The 41/43 "unit-translate" and 2/43 "zero-divisor" cases collapse
into one structural statement:

> **Every non-degenerate violator has a weight-2 element `(α, β) ∈ F_2[G]²`
> with `α · A + β · B = 0`.**

For the 41 cases: `α = 1, β = x^{-h}` (monomials of weight 1 each).
For the 2 cases: `α = 0, β = 1 + x` (weight-0 + weight-2). The 2
outliers can be re-classified: actually, given that `B = y^c (1+x+x²)`,
take `(α, β) = (0, 1+x)`; then `α A + β B = (1+x)·y^c·(1+x+x²) = 0`
because `(1+x)(1+x+x²) = 1 + x³ = 0`. So they fit the same form,
just with one block contributing weight 0 and the other contributing
weight 2.

A cleaner statement: **the violator condition is equivalent to having
a weight-2 element of `ker(H_Z)`**. And that, in turn, is equivalent
to having a low-weight common syzygy in `F_2[G]^2` of `(A, B)`.

## 6. Group structure clustering

Distribution by `group_struct`:
* Z3xZ5: 8 (8/103 = 7.8% violation rate within Z3xZ5 d_exact rows)
* Z5xZ6: 28 (28/2622 = 1.1%)
* Z6xZ6: 7 (7/812 = 0.9%)

The Z3xZ5 violation rate is conspicuously higher because the corpus
has fewer Z3xZ5 instances; the absolute count is also smaller.
**No groups are exempt from violations.**

Notably absent: Z3xZ3, Z3xZ4, Z3xZ6, Z4xZ6 — these groups have **no**
non-degenerate violators despite having non-degenerate rows in the
corpus.

## 7. Are the joint-vanishing orbits unusual?

For every violator, the joint vanishing structure is exactly one
non-trivial orbit:

| group | A | joint-vanishing orbit | size | μ_A | μ_B | contribution |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Z3xZ5 | `y^2+y^3+x` | `{(1,1),(1,4),(2,2),(2,3)}` | 4 | 1 | 1 | 4 |
| Z5xZ6 | `1+xy+x^2` | `{(1,2),(2,1),(3,1),(4,2)}` | 4 | 1 | 1 | 4 |
| Z5xZ6 | `y^3+xy+x^2` | `{(1,2),(2,1),(3,1),(4,2)}` | 4 | 1 | 1 | 4 |
| Z6xZ6 | `1+y+x` | `{(1,2),(2,1)}` | 2 | 2 | 2 | 4 |

So the bound is **uniformly 4**, but `d_exact = 2`. The orbit count
isn't anomalous; the issue is that the dimension-style sum `|O|·μ_O`
doesn't see the weight-2 logical that lives in the *quotient* space
of `ker(H_Z) / rowspan(H_X)`. Confirms §6h of HANDOFF.md: the bound
counts dimensions, not weights, so a "fat" orbit and a "short"
logical can coexist.

---

## 8. Tightening filter candidate

Combining §3, §4, §5: every violator has a weight-2 element in
`ker(H_Z)`. The most natural tightening is

    NEW FILTER:  reject any `(A, B)` such that the BB code `BB(G, A, B)`
                 has a weight-2 codeword in `ker(H_Z)`.

Equivalently, on the polynomial side: reject any pair where there
exists a weight ≤ 2 element `(α, β) ∈ F_2[G]²` with `αA + βB = 0`.

Cost of computing the filter: scan all `|G|^2` pairs `(g, h)` and
test `A · e_g + B · e_h = 0`; scan all weight-2 pure-block elements
(`A · α = 0` for `|α| ≤ 2`, similarly `B · β = 0`). Cheap.

The filter is a **dimension-blind weight test**: it asks "does
`ker(H_Z)` contain a low-weight element?" without invoking any of
the algebraic-decomposition machinery. This is the same shape as
the Tillich-Zémor and Singleton-style bounds: weight-on-the-RHS, not
dimension-on-the-RHS (per §6h's rule).

T3R2.5 below tests this.

---

## 9. Are these codes meaningful?

All 43 violators have `(n, k, d) = (n, 8, 2)`. A distance-2 code
detects but cannot correct single errors — these are operationally
trivial codes. Their inclusion in the corpus is from the broad
enumeration (`bb-lab enumerate --weight 3 --only-k-geq 2`), not
from any practical-interest pre-filter.

So: from a code-design perspective, the filter "no weight-2 logical"
is exactly the filter "use a distance-≥-3 code". The conditional
conjecture restricted to `d ≥ 3` BB codes is therefore essentially
equivalent to "non-degenerate ∧ no weight-2 logical" — and these
violators don't tell us anything useful about engineered BB codes
(gross, the [[72,12,6]] family, etc., which all have d ≥ 6 by
construction).
