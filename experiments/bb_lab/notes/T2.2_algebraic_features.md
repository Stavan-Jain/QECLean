# T2.2 — Algebraic feature deepening

**Goal.** Add features capturing the `F_2[G]` decomposition structure of
the BB-code polynomials `A, B`, so that Tier-2 distance-bound conjectures
have algebraic predictors beyond the existing combinatorial features
(kernel dim, girth, support diameter).

**Hypothesis.** Distance tightness depends on how `A` and `B` distribute
across the irreducible factor components of `F_2[G]`. The new feature
is the **vanishing-on-orbit pattern**: which Frobenius orbits on `Ĝ`
the polynomial vanishes on.

**Not novel.** Frobenius-orbit / cyclotomic-coset decomposition of
`F_p[G]` for finite abelian `G` is the standard Wedderburn / Maschke
construction, treated in Lidl–Niederreiter (*Finite Fields*, Ch. 6),
Curtis–Reiner (*Methods of Representation Theory*, Vol. 1, Ch. 1), and
MacWilliams–Sloane (*Error-Correcting Codes*, Ch. 7). The
Mattson–Solomon transform is the same DFT-over-`F̄_p` reformulation.
We just compute it across the BB corpus to see if vanishing patterns
predict distance.

## Math summary

For finite abelian `G` with `gcd(|G|, 2) = 1`, the group algebra
decomposes (Maschke, Wedderburn) as

    F_2[G]  ≅  ∏_{O ∈ Ĝ // Frobenius}  F_{2^|O|}

one finite-field factor per Frobenius orbit `O` on `Ĝ ≅ G` under
`k ↦ 2k`. Each orbit has size `r = ord_d(2)` where `d` is the order of
any representative; the factor is `F_{2^r}`.

`A ∈ F_2[G]` decomposes coordinatewise, and **`A` vanishes on orbit
`O`** iff its `F_{2^|O|}`-component is zero. The kernel dimension
factors as

    dim ker(M_A)  =  Σ_O |O| · [A vanishes on O]                 (*)

For `|G|` even — e.g. the gross-code group `Z_12 × Z_6`, which has
`|G| = 72 = 8 · 9` — `F_2[G]` is **not semisimple**. The 2-Sylow part
contributes a Jacobson radical (nilpotents), and characters of even
order no longer lift to `F̄_2`. The orbit-vanishing structure on `Ĝ`
captures only the **semisimple quotient** `F_2[G/G[2-Sylow]] =
F_2[G_odd]`, and equation `(*)` undercounts the true `dim ker(M_A)` by
the radical's contribution. The implementation makes this honest: for
even `|G|`, `vanishing_orbits` returns the predicate on the
G_odd-projection (a coarser-but-well-defined grouping) and the docstrings
flag the discrepancy.

## Concrete results on the asked test cases

### `[[30,4,6]]` champion (`G = Z_3 × Z_5`, `A = 1 + x + x^2 y`)

`|G| = 15` (odd, semisimple). Five Frobenius orbits, sizes
`(1, 2, 4, 4, 4)`. `A` vanishes on **exactly one orbit**:
`{(1, 0), (2, 0)}` (size 2), corresponding to the nontrivial cube-root
character of the `x`-axis (the `Z_3` factor). The vanishing signature is
`(2,)`. `dim ker(M_A) = 2`, matching `(*)` exactly.

Algebraic interpretation: `A = 1 + x + x^2 y` is "stuck" in the x-axis
`F_4`-component (where the cube-root substitution forces `A → 0`) but
nowhere else. The kernel of `M_A` is 2-dimensional and lives in this
one component.

### Gross `[[144,12,12]]` (`G = Z_12 × Z_6`, `A = x^3 + y + y^2`)

`|G| = 72 = 8 · 9` (non-semisimple). `G_odd = Z_3 × Z_3` has 5 orbits;
the full `G` partitions into 55 iterate-from-unvisited classes.

**`A` vanishes (in the semisimple sense) on the 3 G_odd-orbits**:
`{(0, 1), (0, 2)}`, `{(1, 1), (2, 2)}`, `{(1, 2), (2, 1)}` — i.e.
**every G_odd-orbit where the y-axis character is nontrivial**. The
trivial orbit `{(0,0)}` and the x-only-nontrivial orbit
`{(1, 0), (2, 0)}` are the **non-vanishing** ones.

Reason: the fiber-sum of `A` to `F_2[G_odd]` is `A_odd = 1 + y + y^2`
(after projecting `x^3 → 1` via the canonical mod-3 surjection). On
G_odd, this collapses to "1 + nontrivial-y-coset-sum" which is `0` in
F̄_2 whenever the y-axis character is a primitive cube root —
exactly the 3 vanishing G_odd-orbits above.

`B = y^3 + x + x^2` mirrors this on the x-axis: `B` vanishes on the
3 G_odd-orbits where the x-axis character is nontrivial:
`{(1, 0), (2, 0)}`, `{(1, 1), (2, 2)}`, `{(1, 2), (2, 1)}`.

**Joint vanishing**: `vanishing(A) ∩ vanishing(B)` in G_odd is the 2
orbits `{(1, 1), (2, 2)}` and `{(1, 2), (2, 1)}` — the "diagonal" and
"anti-diagonal" cube-root characters. This corresponds to the
2-dim-each = 4 dim of `ker(M_A) ∩ ker(M_B)` in the semisimple quotient.

**dim_ker_A discrepancy**: the actual `dim ker(M_A) = 12` (in the corpus,
which is correct) does **not** match `(*)` for gross. The semisimple
formula gives `Σ |O_odd| · [vanishing on O_odd] = 2 + 2 + 2 = 6` (sum
of 3 vanishing G_odd-orbits each of size 2), so the radical of `F_2[G]`
contributes the missing `12 − 6 = 6` dimensions. Per-orbit, this means
each vanishing G_odd-orbit's local-ring contribution to `ker M_A`
is `2 · 2 = 4`, not just the F_4-component dimension `2`. The
multiplicity factor `2` is the nilpotency index of `A` in the
2-Sylow-radical of the local ring.

This is captured **honestly** in the test
`test_gross_vanishing_structure`: we assert the actual `dim ker(M_A) =
12`, verify `A` vanishes on multiple orbits (matching the G_odd picture),
and explicitly check that the orbit-formula `(*)` value is **not** 12
(confirming the radical's presence).

## Corpus-wide observations

Computed `vanishing_orbits(A)`, `vanishing_orbits(B)`, and joint
vanishing over up to 800 randomly sampled rows per group (read-only).

**Observation 1: small `n_vanishing(A)` correlates with higher `d_exact`.**

Per-group, the codes with the FEWEST vanishing orbits tend to have the
HIGHEST distances. Concrete numbers (avg `d_exact` over the sample):

| Group  | top-stratum `n_van`  | avg `d_exact`     | bottom-stratum `n_van` | avg `d_exact` |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------|-----------------------|---------------|
| Z3xZ5  | 1                    | 3.83 (max 6)      | 3                     | 2.00          |
| Z3xZ6  | 3                    | 4.12 (max 6)      | 7                     | 3.12          |
| Z5xZ6  | **2**                | **6.67 (max 8)**  | 11                    | 3.38          |
| Z6xZ6  | 6                    | 5.65 (max 8)      | 18                    | 3.57          |

The Z5xZ6 row is especially clean: `n_van = 2` instances reach `d = 8`
while `n_van = 11` instances cap at `d = 4`. Same direction across all
groups with enough variety to stratify.

**Observation 2: `|joint vanishing|` shows the same pattern, slightly
sharper.**

Same direction (smaller is better) but the strata are slightly
better-separated. For Z5xZ6: `|joint| = 2` reaches `d = 8` (avg
5.59), while `|joint| = 11` caps at `d = 2` (avg 2.0). The joint
predicate filters away cases where one of `A`/`B` vanishes spuriously
on an orbit the other doesn't — likely a noise reduction.

**Observation 3: the vanishing pattern is constant within "rigid" groups.**

Z3xZ4 (`|G| = 12 = 4·3`) and Z4xZ6 (`|G| = 24`) both have all sampled
rows landing on the **same vanishing signature**. These are groups
where the 2-Sylow is large enough that most canonical-form polynomials
are forced into the same algebraic structure. For these groups,
vanishing-orbit-based features are **not discriminative** within the
group — they'd need to be combined with finer features (e.g.
multiplicity-in-radical, which captures the per-orbit "nilpotency
index" beyond just the binary vanishing predicate).

This last point is important for Tier-2: vanishing alone is necessary
but not sufficient. The codes within Z4xZ6 with `d = 4` vs. `d = 2` all
share the same vanishing footprint and must differ in some
finer-grained algebraic invariant (multiplicity in local rings, or
relative position within the semisimple summand).

## Read on signal

`n_vanishing` and `|joint vanishing|` look like **real signal** for
distance prediction within the groups where they vary, and the
direction is consistent (fewer vanishings → higher distance). The
effect is strongest in groups whose `G_odd` is large enough to give
several orbits worth of stratification (`Z3xZ5`, `Z5xZ6`, `Z6xZ6`).

For "4-factor curse" groups (Z3xZ4, Z4xZ6) where the 2-Sylow dominates,
the binary vanishing predicate collapses to a single value across most
canonical instances — a coarse feature that's not useful **on its own**
but might still be useful in interaction with finer features.

For Tier-2 conjecture-mining: the strong univariate correlation
suggests that a refined feature like

    multiplicity-in-radical(A, O)  ∈  {0, 1, ..., 2^{a_O}}

(per-orbit nilpotency index in the local ring, generalizing the binary
vanishing predicate) would likely be a more powerful predictor —
especially for non-semisimple groups. That's a natural follow-up.
