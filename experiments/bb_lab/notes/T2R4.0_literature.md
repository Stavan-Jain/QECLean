**Conclusion: the BBCS framework explicitly requires F_q[G] semisimple
(gcd(|G|, q) = 1).** Since 4 of the 5 Bravyi BB instances (bb_72_12_6,
bb_108_8_10, gross, bb_288_12_18) have even |G| (so 2 divides |G|),
their F_2[G] is non-semisimple and the BBCS framework does **not**

## 1. The target paper

**Bernal, J. J., Bueno-Carreño, D. H., Simón, J. J. (2016).**
*Apparent Distance and a Notion of BCH Multivariate Codes.*
IEEE Trans. Inform. Theory **62**(2), 655–668.
[DOI 10.1109/TIT.2015.2512594](https://doi.org/10.1109/TIT.2015.2512594).

arXiv preprint update (Feb 2024): [arXiv:2402.03938](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.03938).
Both versions have identical mathematical content; the 2024 upload is
just a cleaner typesetting.

### What the paper does

Given an abelian code `C ⊆ A_q(r_1, …, r_s) ≅ F_q[X_1,…,X_s] / (X_1^{r_1}-1, …, X_s^{r_s}-1)`,
the *apparent distance* `d^*(C)` (originally Camion 1971) is a lower
bound on the minimum Hamming distance of C, computed from C's defining
set (the support of the inverse-Fourier image of an idempotent).

The 2016 paper introduces:

1. The notion of the *apparent distance of a hypermatrix* (extending
   Sabin–Lomonaco's 1992 matrix manipulations to dimension s ≥ 2).
2. An algorithm to compute the *minimum apparent distance* `mad(M)` of
   a q-orbits hypermatrix M, via iterative "involved hypercolumn"
   discrepancy sequences (Theorem 22).
3. The fact that `d^*(C) = max_α { mad(M(φ_{α,e})) : α ∈ U }` where
   `e` is the generating idempotent of C and `φ_{α,e}` is its discrete
   Fourier transform (Theorem 12).
4. A reduction of the computational complexity from exponential
   `2^μ - 1` polynomial-apparent-distance computations (Sabin 1992,
   Camion 1971) down to linear in μ in the bivariate case (Remark 20).
5. A notion of *BCH bound* and *BCH code* in the multivariate setting
   (Section V), generalizing the classical BCH machinery.

The hypermatrix-manipulation algorithm cleanly handles **non-cyclic**
abelian G (e.g. `Z_3 × Z_3`, `Z_3 × Z_5`); this is the headline
contribution we hoped would lift Round 3's "G_odd non-cyclic"
obstruction.

### Critical assumption — F_q[G] semisimple

The paper states the assumption explicitly:

> **(Page 2, Section II "Notation and preliminaries")**
> "*We deal with abelian codes in the semisimple case; that is, we
> always assume that gcd(r_k, q) = 1 for every k = 1, …, s.*"

It is reiterated at the start of Section III:

> **(Page 3, Section III opening)**
> "*Throughout this section s, q and r_1, …, r_s will be positive
> integers, with q a power of a prime number p, such that p ∤ r_i,
> for i = 1, …, s.*"

And again in Theorem 25 (the multivariate BCH bound) and Theorem 31
(the dimension bound).

Several internal lemmas depend on this assumption structurally — the
generating idempotent of a non-semisimple group algebra ideal does
not decompose into primitive central idempotents in the same way
(the radical contributes additional, non-idempotent generators).
Theorem 12 (the apparent-distance-from-Fourier-transform identity)
relies on this decomposition.

### Cross-check: the 2017 companion paper

Bernal, Guerreiro, Simón (2017),
*"From ds-bounds for cyclic codes to true distance for abelian codes,"*
[arXiv:1704.03761](https://arxiv.org/abs/1704.03761) (ISITA 2016).

Same authors, same framework, extends the apparent-distance toolkit
to general ds-bounds (HT, Roos, Van Lint–Wilson shifting bound, etc.).
The assumption is identical and stated more emphatically:

> **(Page 3, Section 2 "Preliminaries")**
> "*An abelian code of length n is an ideal in the algebra
> F_q(r_1, …, r_s) = F_q[X_1, …, X_s] / ⟨X_1^{r_1} − 1, …, X_s^{r_s} − 1⟩
> and **throughout this work we assume that this algebra is
> semisimple; that is, gcd(r_i, q) = 1, for all i ∈ {1, …, s}**.*"

So the entire BBCS-line of work (2011 → 2016 → 2017 → 2024 follow-up
papers) bakes the semisimplicity assumption into its preliminaries.

## 2. Why the assumption is not just a convenience

The "discrete Fourier transform of an idempotent → hypermatrix"
construction in BBCS is **not** literally a vector-space Fourier
transform; it is a decomposition of `F_q[G]` into a product of
extension fields `∏ F_{q^v_α}` (one per q-orbit of characters), one
factor per primitive central idempotent in the semisimple Wedderburn
decomposition.

When `gcd(|G|, q) > 1`, the Wedderburn decomposition does not exist
over `F_q[G]`; instead, F_q[G] is a product of *local* (non-semisimple)
rings, each with a Jacobson radical. The "characters" living in
the radical are *not* visible to any DFT-based construction, and
the bound `d^*(C)` defined via Definition 9 of the 2016 paper would
not even be well-defined (the generating idempotent `e` of an ideal
in a non-semisimple ring may not lift to a sum of primitive central
idempotents).

To put numbers on this for gross: `G = Z_12 × Z_6`, `|G| = 72`,
`q = 2`. The 2-Sylow of G has order 8 (a quotient of order 8 in
G; structurally Z_4 × Z_2). So F_2[G] is a product of 9 local rings
(one per 2-regular orbit of G_odd = Z_3 × Z_3), each of which has
Jacobson radical of dimension 7 (the order of the 2-Sylow minus 1).
The semisimple quotient F_2[G_odd] sees only 9 of the 72 dimensions.

This is the same algebraic obstruction Round 3's `T2R3.4_eval.md`
documents under "diagnostic = G_not_semisimple"; BBCS does not help
us see past the F_2[G_odd] quotient.

## 3. Companion literature (which also doesn't help)

### 3a. Jitman & Ling (2013), "Abelian Codes in Principal Ideal Group Algebras"
([IEEE Xplore](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/6400250))

This paper explicitly addresses the non-semisimple case (PIGA = a
group algebra F[G] whose two-sided ideals are all principal, which
in the abelian case means F[G] is a product of local chain rings).
Two adverse findings:

1. An upper bound for the minimum distance of abelian codes in a
   non-semisimple PIGA is **derived from** the minimum distance of
   abelian codes in semisimple group algebras (specifically the
   semisimple quotient F[G/Sylow_p(G)]). So any non-semisimple bound
   they provide is at most the semisimple-quotient bound.

2. Abelian codes in non-semisimple PIGAs are shown to be
   "**asymptotically bad**" (in the same sense as repeated-root
   cyclic codes), i.e. they cannot maintain `d/n → δ > 0` as `n → ∞`.

Both findings reinforce that the algebra of F_2[G] with `2 | |G|` is
fundamentally inhospitable to the *single-block* cyclic-code distance
approach. BB codes' good distance scaling comes from the *coupling*
between blocks A and B (the lifted-product wraparound), not from
either block's standalone classical distance.

### 3b. Camion (1971), *Abelian Codes* (MRC Tech. Sum. Rep. 1059)

The original apparent-distance reference. Semisimple-case only (the
construction goes via the multiplicative-character group of G, which
requires `gcd(|G|, q) = 1`).

### 3c. Sabin & Lomonaco (1992), Sabin (1992) on bivariate-cyclic apparent distance
*("On Minimum Distance Bounds for Abelian Codes")*

Same semisimple assumption (this is the precursor that BBCS 2016
generalizes to multivariate via hypermatrices).

### 3d. Saints & Heegard (1995), Lalaude-Labayle (2001), de la Vega-Lopez (2015)
on multivariate-cyclic-code BCH-style bounds — all in the semisimple
case. The non-semisimple case is mentioned occasionally as "future
work" but no concrete weight-invariant lower bound exists in the
literature we surveyed.

### 3e. Castagnoli–Massey–Schoeller–von Seemann (1991)
on *repeated-root cyclic codes* (the univariate analogue of
non-semisimple abelian codes). CMS 1991 show the minimum distance of
a repeated-root cyclic code is exactly the minimum distance of its
simple-root reduction times a multiplicity factor (their P_t
structure; Berman 1967 is the precursor). *(Attribution corrected
2026-06 per A1 lane L1: this entry previously co-credited "Roth &
Seroussi (1986)", whose paper is the unrelated "On cyclic MDS codes
of length q over GF(q)" — not about repeated roots.)* This is
the same "non-semisimple bound = derived from semisimple bound"
phenomenon — but note the 2026-06 re-reading in HANDOFF §6j: the
multiplicative factor means non-semisimple distances can EXCEED the
bare semisimple-quotient distance, which is exactly the mechanism
Track A wants to exploit, not a uselessness verdict.

## 4. Property checklist for BBCS

Per the T2R4 spec:

| Property | BBCS handles? | Evidence |
|---|:---:|---|
| (a) Non-cyclic G (gcd(ℓ, m) > 1) | **YES** | Hypermatrix algorithm, Example 23 (`Z_3 × Z_3 × Z_5`), etc. |
| (b) Non-semisimple F₂[G] (2 \| \|G\|) | **NO** | Page 2, page 3, Theorem 22, Theorem 25 all assume `p ∤ r_i`. |
| (c) Degenerate codes (`[G:⟨supp(A)⟩] > 1`) | partially | The framework operates on the defining set of C, which is permutation-invariant; degeneracy in the literal HANDOFF §6i sense is a property of the polynomial A, not of the abelian code C. In principle BBCS doesn't care about it — but in practice, when supp(A) generates a strict subgroup H < G, the code C generated by A has the same defining set in F_q[G] as in F_q[H], so BBCS just sees the smaller-group problem. This doesn't help BB-code distance because BB's coupling between A and B operates over G, not H. |

**Property (b) is the deal-breaker.** The T2R4 spec explicitly says:
"*If the paper turns out to NOT handle non-semisimple F₂[G] (which would
re-instate the Round 3 obstruction), document this and stop.*"

Four of the five Bravyi instances have `2 | |G|`:

| code | |G| | even? | F_2[G] semisimple? |
|---|---:|:---:|:---:|
| bb_72_12_6 | 36 | yes | NO |
| bb_90_8_10 | 45 | no  | yes |
| bb_108_8_10 | 54 | yes | NO |
| gross | 72 | yes | **NO** |
| bb_288_12_18 | 144 | yes | NO |

The one instance where F_2[G] *is* semisimple (bb_90, `|G| = 45`)
has G_odd = G = Z_15 × Z_3 (which is non-cyclic since gcd(15, 3) = 3
— wait, gcd(15, 3) = 3, but Z_15 × Z_3 isn't cyclic because lcm(15, 3)
= 15 < 45 = |G|; the cyclic structure of Z_15 × Z_3 would require
gcd(15, 3) = 1). For bb_90 alone BBCS *could* fire — it satisfies (a)
and (b). But this is a single data point and the spec target is gross.

## 5. Could BBCS still help indirectly?

Two paths we considered before concluding "stop":

### 5a. Apply BBCS to F_2[G_odd] for the four non-semisimple Bravyi instances

BBCS would give a lower bound on the minimum distance of the abelian
code in F_2[G_odd] generated by the projection of A. But:

1. This is exactly the semisimple-quotient code that Round 3's HT/Roos
   bound *already* applies to. BBCS at best gives a better number for
   the same quotient, but the quotient itself is the wrong target —
   the F_2[G] kernel (`ker M_A`) contains low-weight vectors *invisible*
   to the F_2[G_odd] quotient (these are exactly the radical-contributed
   kernel elements). So `d_A^⊥(F_2[G]) ≤ d_A^⊥(F_2[G_odd])` strictly,
   and BBCS-on-quotient cannot lower-bound `d_X(BB)` any better than HT.

2. Empirically (Round 3 T2R3.4): even on the semisimple quotient, the
   HT bound returns 1 for gross's A polynomial. So a sharper bound on
   the same quotient would have to give >1; whether BBCS does this for
   gross's particular `T_A` in F_2[Z_3 × Z_3] is testable, but the
   single-block-dominance condition `S(A, B)` is **also** known to
   fail on gross (per T2R3.4 §1: `S(A, B)? = no (G_not_semisimple)`).
   So even if BBCS gave a sharp bound on the F_2[Z_3 × Z_3] quotient,
   we cannot lift it to `d_X(BB) ≥ ⋯` without S holding.

### 5b. Use BBCS as a "feature" rather than as the bound itself

Use BBCS-apparent-distance on the F_2[G_odd] quotient as a feature
to discriminate which BB codes might have small d, then build a
separate weight invariant for the radical contribution. This is what
HANDOFF §6i recommends in spirit ("use degeneracy as a feature, not
as a domain restriction"). But this means BBCS is not itself the
bound — at most a component. The current round's deliverable
"`bb_apparent_bound(A, B, G)` returning a lower bound on `d_X`"
cannot be honestly produced by BBCS alone.

## 6. Verdict

**Per the T2R4 spec, the literature check resolves to: stop.**

The BBCS apparent-distance framework explicitly excludes the
non-semisimple case (assumption `gcd(r_k, q) = 1` is stated at least
4 times across page 2, page 3, Section III, Section V of the 2016
paper; identical assumption in the 2017 companion paper). 4 of 5
Bravyi codes — including gross — have F_2[G] non-semisimple, so BBCS
cannot produce a lower bound on `d_X(BB)` for them.

The follow-up Jitman–Ling 2013 paper on non-semisimple PIGAs shows
abelian-code distance bounds in the non-semisimple regime are
**derived from** the semisimple quotient (never sharper), and that
the codes themselves are asymptotically bad in this setting. So this
is not a "missing paper" obstruction (where a different reference
would unblock us) — it is a structural obstruction to the entire
character-theoretic / Fourier-transform / apparent-distance line of
classical multivariate-abelian-code algebra.

The 4-tier program's verdict for this round is therefore:
**`shelved-paper-doesnt-help`** (the spec's explicit branch for
this case).

The right next direction (per HANDOFF §6i and the Round 3 result.md
"What survives") is a **bound that explicitly handles the radical
of F_2[G] for even |G|**, or a **non-algebraic / homological / cover-
graph approach** (e.g. Pesah et al. 2025 lifted-product covers).
Both are beyond the scope of "port the BBCS algorithm" and would
constitute a separate Tier 2 round.
